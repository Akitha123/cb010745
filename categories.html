Turtle Species

Green Turtle





In Sri Lanka's coastal seas, the green turtle (Chelonia mydas) has a special place in people's
hearts. The marine ecology of the island nation is dominated and beloved by these amazing species.
Green turtles, distinguished by their vivid greenish-brown shells, are regularly spotted in the warm
waters around Sri Lanka.The sandy beaches of Sri Lanka serve as crucial nesting sites for green turtles.
Female turtles return to these shores, specifically in places like Rekawa, Kosgoda, and Pigeon Island, to lay their eggs. It is a remarkable sight to witness these gentle giants emerging from the ocean,
laboriously digging nests in the sand, and carefully depositing their eggs before returning to the sea. In Sri Lanka's coastal seas, the green turtle (Chelonia mydas) is revered. The island nation's marine
ecosystem is home to these majestic creatures, which are a prominent and beloved sight.
Green turtles are common in the warm waters around Sri Lanka and are distinguished by their vivid
greenish-brown shells.However, these incredible creatures face various threats in Sri Lanka,
including habitat degradation, pollution, fishing-related activities, and illegal poaching. Conservation
efforts, such as marine protected areas, awareness campaigns, and community involvement, are crucial for the
conservation of green turtles in Sri Lanka.You can easily spot Green Turtle in Rekawa Beach.



Hawksbill Turtle






The Hawksbill turtle, Eretmochelys imbricata in scientific jargon, is a fascinating and seriously
endangered species that inhabits Sri Lanka's coastal waters. These turtles possess significant
ecological and cultural value in the nation and are well-known for their distinctive and elaborate shell
patterns. Hawksbill turtles use Sri Lanka as a significant nesting area, and they choose to lay their eggs
on a number of beaches throughout the country's eastern and southern coasts. Female turtles emerge from the
ocean between November and April, where they meticulously dig nests in the sand to lay their eggs.
The turtles' smooth migration from the water to the beach and back during this breeding phase is an amazing
sight to behold. The existence of hawksbill turtles, however, is threatened by various factors. Some of the
biggest problems they face include the erosion of nesting grounds, pollution, climate change, and unintentional
catch in fishing nets. A major threat to their number is also posed by the illicit trade in their exquisite shells
and eggs.Sri Lanka has developed a number of conservation initiatives to combat these risks and safeguard
Hawksbill turtles. Turtle eggs are collected and incubated at hatcheries along the coast, where they are given
a safer environment to hatch. In order to increase the hatchlings' chances of survival, they are then released
into the sea under carefully monitored conditionsFurthermore, community-based initiatives and conservation
organizations actively work to raise awareness about the importance of protecting these endangered creatures.
Educational programs, beach clean-ups, and responsible tourism practices are promoted to minimize human impact
on nesting sites and turtle habitats.. You can easily spot Green Turtle in Panama Beach.



Olive Ridley






The fascinating marine species known as the Olive Ridley turtle, or Lepidochelys olivacea,
can be found in Sri Lanka's coastal waters. One of the smallest sea turtle species, these turtles are
distinguished for their olive-colored carapace, which gives them their name. Olive ridley turtles use
Sri Lanka as a significant nesting location, and their arribadas, or mass nesting occasions, are
breathtaking to witness. In coordinated nesting rituals, thousands of female turtles come ashore at the
same time to lay their eggs. These arribadas are not only an amazing natural occurrence, but they also show
how vulnerable these turtles are while they are nesting. Despite their ability to gather in large numbers,
Olive ridley turtles face significant threats. Habitat destruction, pollution, entanglement in fishing gear,
and illegal harvesting of their eggs and meat are some of the main challenges they encounter. Climate change
also poses a threat, as it affects nesting habitats and alters ocean currents and temperatures.To protect
Olive ridley turtles, Sri Lanka has implemented various conservation measures. Turtle conservation projects,
hatcheries, and community-led initiatives play a crucial role in safeguarding these endangered reptiles.
Conservationists and volunteers patrol nesting beaches, protect nests from predation, and raise awareness about
the importance of preserving these turtles and their habitats.When planning a visit to these locations,
it's important to check the nesting season, as Hawksbill turtles typically nest between November and April in
Sri Lanka. Additionally, it is crucial to follow responsible guidelines and respect the turtles and their nesting
sites to minimize disturbance and ensure their well-being.You can easily spot Green Turtle in Pottuvil.



Loggerhead Turtle






The loggerhead turtle (Caretta caretta) holds a significant presence in the coastal waters
of Sri Lanka, making it an important part of the country's marine ecosystem. Sri Lanka's warm tropical
waters provide a favorable environment for these majestic creatures. Loggerhead turtles are known to
frequent the sandy beaches of Sri Lanka for nesting, contributing to the island's rich biodiversity. Sri Lanka serves as a crucial nesting site for loggerhead turtles, particularly along its southern and
eastern coasts. The nesting season, which typically occurs between November and April, sees female
loggerheads emerge from the depths of the Indian Ocean to lay their eggs. Under the cover of darkness,
they navigate the sandy shores with precision, searching for suitable spots to dig their nests. The process
of nesting is a delicate and vulnerable period for these turtles, as they depend on the protection of
undisturbed beaches to ensure successful reproduction.You can easily spot Green Turtle in Koggala Beach.



Leatherback Turtle






The endangered leatherback turtle (Dermochelys coriacea) is distinguished by its distinctive leathery shell. The Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans are just a few places where the leatherback turtle can be found, but Sri Lanka is where it is known to lay its eggs.Leatherback turtles frequently lay their eggs in Sri Lanka, especially on the beaches along the country's eastern and southern shores. Leatherback turtles congregate in great numbers to lay their eggs on certain beaches and are referred to as "arribada," which means mass nesting. Typically, this event takes place between April and July, which is the breeding season.These enormous turtles, which may grow to a length of 7 feet (2.13 meters) and weigh more than 1,000 kilograms (2,200 pounds), are the biggest sea turtles. They get their name from the peculiar shell structure of their flexible, rubbery skin. The leatherback turtle lacks a hard, bony shell in contrast to other marine turtles.Given the multiple risks to leatherback turtles, including habitat loss, pollution, climate change, and fishing activities, conservation efforts are essential. By creating marine protected areas, putting rules in place to lessen disturbance to nesting locations, and spreading awareness of the value of protecting leatherback turtles and their habitats, Sri Lanka has taken steps to safeguard these endangered species.
