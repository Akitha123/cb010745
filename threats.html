Threats to Sea Turtles

Illigal Fishing

Illegal fishing poses a significant threat to turtles in Sri Lanka. The use of destructive fishing practices, such as trawling nets and longlines, can lead to the accidental capture and entanglement of turtles. This can result in injury or death, as turtles are unable to reach the surface to breathe. Additionally, the consumption of turtle meat and eggs remains a challenge, as it continues to drive illegal fishing activities.







Artificial lighting

Artificial lighting along coastal areas is a major concern for turtles. When hatchlings emerge from their nests, they rely on natural light reflections from the moon and stars to guide them towards the ocean. However, artificial lights can disorient hatchlings, causing them to move away from the sea and towards roads, buildings, or other dangerous areas. This can result in high mortality rates, as hatchlings become vulnerable to predation, dehydration, or exhaustion.



Oil spills and marine pollution

Oil spills have devastating effects on marine ecosystems, including turtles. When oil is released into the water, it forms a slick on the surface, making it difficult for turtles to breathe and move freely. Turtles that come into contact with oil can experience oil ingestion or inhalation, leading to internal damage and contamination. The toxic effects of oil can harm their organs, impair their immune systems, and disrupt their reproductive capabilities.
Marine pollution, including plastic debris and chemical pollutants, poses a significant threat to turtles in Sri Lanka. Turtles often mistake plastic bags, bottles, and other debris for food, which can result in ingestion and intestinal blockage. Chemical pollutants, such as pesticides and heavy metals, accumulate in their bodies, leading to health issues and reproductive problems. The degradation of habitats due to pollution also impacts nesting grounds and foraging areas, further endangering turtle populations.
